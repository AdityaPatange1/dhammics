*Metta* is sometimes translated as "loving-kindness," but the translation can mislead. It is
not the warm feeling you have when something goes your way. It is a *practice* — the patient
training of a heart that remains friendly in every direction, including toward the people
who make that difficult.

## The classical framework

The traditional metta practice begins close in, where warmth comes naturally, and works
outward in widening circles:

1. **Yourself.** Most Western practitioners find this the hardest. Begin anyway.
2. **A benefactor** — someone whose kindness is unmistakable.
3. **A dear friend.**
4. **A neutral person** — someone you neither like nor dislike.
5. **A difficult person.** (Start with a mildly difficult one.)
6. **All beings, in every direction, without exception.**

## The phrases

The phrases are scaffolding. Use them until you don't need them; then return to them when
the heart cools. Any short, honest wish works:

- May you be safe.
- May you be healthy.
- May you be happy.
- May you live with ease.

Say them silently, in rhythm with the breath. Notice what arises when you offer each phrase
to each person. Resentment, boredom, tenderness, doubt — they are all *part* of the practice,
not interruptions to it.

## When metta stalls

Sometimes the heart refuses to warm. This is not a moral failure; it is information. A few
things that can help:

- **Drop the effort to feel a feeling.** Just offer the wish. Let the heart do what it does.
- **Return to the benefactor.** Replenish. Then widen again.
- **Investigate the closed place.** What would need to be true for this person to have acted
  differently? Almost always, what is missing is a condition you would also have missed.
- **Practise compassion (karuna) instead.** For now, wish only that their suffering ease.
  This is often easier when goodwill feels forced.

## Outside the cushion

Metta that lives only in meditation is not yet metta. Carry one phrase with you through a
day. Offer it silently to the barista, the driver who cuts you off, the colleague whose
opinions you find unbearable. You do not have to approve of anyone to wish them well.

> "Just as a mother would protect with her life her own son, her only son, so toward all
> beings let one cultivate a boundless heart." — *Metta Sutta*
