Sitting meditation gets most of the attention, but the Buddha encouraged a balanced
practice: sitting, standing, walking, lying down. Walking meditation keeps the body
from stiffening, clears drowsiness, and — more importantly — trains the mind to stay
present while the body is moving. That second skill is the one we use most of the day.

## Setting up

Find a stretch of ground, indoors or out, about ten to twenty paces long. It can be shorter.
It can be a hallway. Walk a little slower than your natural pace, but not so slowly that you
wobble.

Let the arms hang, or clasp the hands loosely in front or behind. Keep the eyes soft, looking
a few steps ahead. The body is alert; the face is at ease.

## The practice

There are many variants. One simple sequence:

1. **Notice the whole body standing.** Feel the weight pressing into the ground.
2. **Begin to walk.** Let the attention rest at the soles of the feet.
3. **Lifting, moving, placing.** Mentally note each phase of the step, in rhythm with the
   actual movement — not faster, not slower.
4. **At the end of the lane, stop.** Stand for a breath. Turn. Notice the turning.
5. **Walk back.**

When the mind wanders — to what's for dinner, to an old argument — note "thinking," and
return to the feet. Gently, always gently.

## Why walk

For beginners and long-time practitioners alike, walking meditation offers three gifts that
sitting does not:

- **Continuity.** It is easier to keep attention on an *object* when that object is moving.
- **Integration.** The habit of presence in motion is the habit we most need in daily life.
- **Physical ease.** Walking works out the stiffness that can build up over long retreats.

> "When walking, he discerns, 'I am walking.' When standing, he discerns, 'I am standing.'"
> — *Satipatthana Sutta*

## A quiet test

Try this tomorrow. For the first three minutes of a daily walk — the walk to the bus, to the
car, to the next meeting — give attention to the soles of the feet. No chants, no phrases,
no analysis. Just the contact with the ground. See what you notice about the rest of the
day.
