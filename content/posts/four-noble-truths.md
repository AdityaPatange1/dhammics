After his awakening in the shade of the Bodhi tree, the Buddha is said to have hesitated. The
truth he had seen was *deep*, *subtle*, *against the stream*. Could it be taught at all? He
walked to Sarnath, sat among five former companions, and offered what has since been called
the first turning of the wheel: the **Four Noble Truths**.

They are not articles of belief. They are a physician's framework.

## The first truth: there is dukkha

*Dukkha* is often translated as "suffering," but the word is wider and quieter than that. It
names the subtle unsatisfactoriness that threads through ordinary experience — the way even
pleasure leaves behind a faint ache, the way everything we love is exactly the sort of thing
that can be taken from us.

> "Birth is dukkha, aging is dukkha, death is dukkha; sorrow, lamentation, pain, grief, and
> despair are dukkha; union with what is displeasing is dukkha; separation from what is
> pleasing is dukkha; not to get what one wants is dukkha." — *Dhammacakkappavattana Sutta*

![Morning mist over still water](https://images.unsplash.com/photo-1501785888041-af3ef285b470?auto=format&fit=crop&w=1600&q=80)

To see this clearly is not pessimism. It is honesty. Only when we stop pretending that a
slightly better arrangement of conditions will finally satisfy us, can we turn toward the
question that actually matters.

## The second truth: there is a cause

The cause of dukkha is *tanha* — thirst, craving, the compulsive leaning of the mind toward
what it wants and away from what it does not. It has three flavours: craving for sense
pleasures, craving for existence, and craving for non-existence.

Notice how intimate this is. The cause is not out there in the world. It is a movement in
the mind, arising again and again, fabricating a sense of a self who must grasp in order to be safe.

## The third truth: cessation is possible

This is the hinge of the entire teaching: craving is *conditioned*, and what is conditioned
can end. When the mind stops grasping — even for a breath — suffering does not arise. This
is *nibbana*, the going-out of the fire.

The third truth is not a promise deferred to some distant future. It is a discovery
available now, in small moments, when attention rests and craving quiets.

## The fourth truth: there is a path

The path is the **Noble Eightfold Path** — right view, right intention, right speech, right
action, right livelihood, right effort, right mindfulness, right concentration. Each of
these we explore in separate essays; together they form a complete way of living, not a
checklist.

---

The Four Noble Truths are usually introduced as a doctrine to be memorised. Try holding them,
instead, as four invitations:

1. **Turn toward the ache** — don't paper over it.
2. **Look for the craving** that arises with it.
3. **Notice** when the craving eases, even briefly.
4. **Walk the path** that lets that easing become a way of life.

That is the whole Dhamma, in a first breath.
