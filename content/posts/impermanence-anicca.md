A bell rings. For a breath, the room is full of sound. Then the tone thins, fades, and is
gone. We did not have to *do* anything for the sound to disappear. Its ending was already
present in its beginning.

This is *anicca* — impermanence — and it is one of the three marks the Buddha pointed to
again and again: *anicca, dukkha, anatta*. Everything that is compounded is impermanent.
Everything that is impermanent is, at some level, unsatisfying to cling to. And nothing that
we would cling to is a fixed, abiding self.

## Not a pessimism

Western ears sometimes hear "everything is impermanent" and brace for tragedy. But the
teaching is not that life is a long goodbye. It is that the *clinging* which pretends things
are more solid than they are, is what aches.

![Autumn leaves on flowing water](https://images.unsplash.com/photo-1507041957456-9c397ce39c97?auto=format&fit=crop&w=1600&q=80)

Seen clearly, impermanence is what makes anything possible. A breath can end so the next can
begin. A mood can lift. A wound can heal. A grief can soften. An old self can be laid down.

## Looking at the three time-scales

Try this as a sitting exercise. Over one period of meditation, notice impermanence on three
scales:

- **The long** — the body, older than last year. Relationships that have changed. Views that
  used to seem obvious and don't anymore.
- **The medium** — the emotional weather of the past hour. The way boredom gave way to
  interest, interest to sleepiness, sleepiness to a quiet clarity.
- **The brief** — a single breath. A single thought, arriving, being known, departing. The
  flicker of an itch.

All three are the same fact, looked at from different distances.

## The practical gift

When we stop expecting permanence where there is none, something eases. We love without
grasping quite so hard. We grieve without being surprised. We praise without inflating. We
criticise without becoming bitter. The heart becomes, slowly, a little more *willing*.

> "All conditioned things are impermanent. When one sees this with wisdom, one turns away
> from suffering. This is the path of purification." — *Dhammapada 277*

The practice is not to reach a place where change does not hurt. The practice is to stop
asking change not to happen.
