There is a reason the Buddha returned to the breath again and again. It is always with us; it
is always changing; it is equally available to the farmer and the philosopher. And when we
sit with it quietly, something improbable happens — the mind, which had seemed so chaotic,
begins to settle.

## Simply arrive

Find a posture in which you can be *alert* and *at ease*. Not a statue, not a slouch. Let the
spine lift, the shoulders soften, the hands rest. Close the eyes, or leave them half-open,
gazing unfocused at the floor.

![Hands resting on the knees in seated meditation](https://images.unsplash.com/photo-1545205597-3d9d02c29597?auto=format&fit=crop&w=1600&q=80)

Do not yet try to meditate. Simply notice that you are breathing. Then — slowly — turn
attention to *one* place where the breath is most vivid for you. The nostrils. The upper lip.
The rise and fall of the belly.

## Four steps, sixteen contemplations

The *Ānāpānasati Sutta* outlines sixteen contemplations in four sets. For a first practice,
the first four are enough:

1. Breathing in long, know "long". Breathing out long, know "long".
2. Breathing in short, know "short". Breathing out short, know "short".
3. Breathing in, *experience the whole body*. Breathing out, *experience the whole body*.
4. Breathing in, calming the body. Breathing out, calming the body.

Notice the progression. First we simply *know* the breath. Then we *sense it bodily*. Then we
*let the body calm itself* in rhythm with the breath. Nothing is forced. The calming happens
because attention has stopped agitating it.

## When the mind wanders

It will. This is not a failure. It is the practice. The moment you notice you have wandered
is a moment of mindfulness already arisen. You do not need to punish the mind or grip it
harder. Simply return. The gentleness *is* the training.

> "Breathing in, I know I am breathing in. Breathing out, I know I am breathing out. This is
> all — and this is everything."

## A five-minute version

If sitting feels impossible today, try this in your chair, right now:

1. Take one deliberate in-breath. Notice the cool air at the nostrils.
2. Take one deliberate out-breath. Notice the warmer air leaving.
3. Repeat twenty times. Count only if it helps.
4. When the twentieth breath is done, do *not* get up immediately. Notice what changed.

That small pause at the end — the noticing — is often where the practice deposits itself.
